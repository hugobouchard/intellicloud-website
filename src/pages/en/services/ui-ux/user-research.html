Our User Research Solution

✓ Discovery Research

Understand user needs before building anything. Conduct user interviews (20-30 participants across segments), analyze behavior with analytics, map user journeys identifying pain points. Create personas grounded in real data, not assumptions. Validate problems worth solving.

✓ Usability Testing

Test whether users can actually use your product. Moderated sessions with 5-8 users per segment (uncovers 85% of usability issues), task-based scenarios with success metrics, think-aloud protocol capturing confusion points. Unmoderated testing for larger sample sizes and quantitative data.

✓ Analytics & A/B Testing

Quantitative validation at scale. Analyze user behavior data identifying drop-off points, design and run A/B tests with statistical rigor, track metrics that actually matter (activation, retention, revenue—not just clicks). Turn data into actionable insights.

✓ Actionable Insights & Recommendations

Research means nothing without action. Synthesize findings into clear insights, prioritize recommendations by impact, present findings with video clips and quotes (stakeholders remember stories, not stats), create specific design/product recommendations, track which insights led to changes.

Case Study: B2B SaaS Onboarding Research

The Challenge

B2B SaaS company had 58% of trial users never complete onboarding. Product team assumed users didn't understand value proposition. Kept redesigning landing page and adding more features. Churn remained high. No user research conducted—just assumptions and opinions.

Our Solution

Conducted 25 user interviews with churned and successful users. Usability testing revealed real problem: initial setup required 8 separate integrations—overwhelming and time-consuming. Analytics showed 67% drop-off at integration step. Recommended simplified onboarding with progressive setup. A/B tested new flow showing 2.4x improvement.

Our User Research Process

1

Research Planning & Strategy

Understand your research questions and business goals, define what you need to learn (problem discovery vs. solution validation), choose appropriate research methods (qualitative vs. quantitative), identify user segments to study, create recruiting screener and discussion guide.

2

Participant Recruitment

Recruit from your existing users, use professional recruiting panels (UserTesting, Respondent.io), post in relevant communities and forums, run intercept surveys for in-product recruitment. Screen participants to ensure they match target profile. Typically recruit 5-8 per segment for qualitative, 100+ for quantitative.

3

Research Execution

Conduct user interviews (60-90 minutes each), run usability tests with think-aloud protocol, analyze behavioral data and funnels, design and execute A/B tests or surveys. Record sessions (with permission), take detailed notes, observe non-verbal cues. Stakeholders can observe live sessions.

4

Analysis & Synthesis

Transcribe and code interview data, identify patterns and themes across participants, map findings to user journeys, create affinity diagrams grouping related insights. Separate observations from insights from recommendations. Quantify where possible (e.g., "7 of 8 users couldn't find X").

5

Insights & Recommendations

Present findings to stakeholders with video highlights and direct quotes, prioritize recommendations by user impact and implementation effort, create specific design/product recommendations (not just "improve UX"), define success metrics to track if changes work. Follow up to track which insights led to product decisions.

Frequently Asked Questions

What types of user research do you conduct?

User interviews (discovery and validation), usability testing (moderated and unmoderated), surveys and questionnaires, analytics analysis, A/B testing and experimentation, card sorting and tree testing for information architecture, competitive analysis, user journey mapping, and persona development. We select methods based on your research questions.

How many users do we need for research?

For qualitative research (interviews, usability testing): 5-8 users per segment typically uncovers 85% of usability issues. For quantitative research (surveys, A/B tests): depends on statistical significance needs, typically 100+ responses for surveys, 1000+ users per variant for A/B tests with measurable impact.

How do you recruit research participants?

Use your existing customer base (easiest for retention/experience research), recruit via professional panels (UserTesting, Respondent.io), social media and community forums, intercept surveys for in-product recruitment, and work with specialized recruiters for hard-to-reach segments (enterprise buyers, doctors, etc.). Always screen participants to ensure they match your target user profile.

What's the difference between user research and user testing?

User research is broader—understanding user needs, behaviors, and motivations through interviews, surveys, contextual inquiry, etc. User testing is more specific—evaluating whether users can successfully use your product through usability testing, A/B testing, etc. Discovery research happens before design, validation testing happens after. Both are valuable at different product stages.

How do you turn research insights into product decisions?

Synthesize findings into actionable insights (not just observations), map insights to product opportunities and pain points, prioritize based on user impact and implementation effort, create specific recommendations with supporting evidence (video clips, quotes, data), present findings to stakeholders compellingly, and track which insights led to product changes and measure the impact of those changes.
